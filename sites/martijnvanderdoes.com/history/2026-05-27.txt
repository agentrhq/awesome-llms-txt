URL: https://martijnvanderdoes.com/#journal
Author: Martijn van der Does
Published: 2025-06-24
Section: Journal
Summary: Argues that competitive advantage in AI comes from organizational culture design, talent attraction, and strategic systems rather than just technological capabilities.
Tags: AI-culture, talent-design, organizational-strategy, human-AI-collaboration

Full Text:
I've been thinking about the AI gold rush lately, not about the models or compute, but about the people and systems we're building around them.

After watching billion-dollar acqui-hires and $100M signing bonuses make headlines, I keep coming back to one question: What happens when the models become commoditized?

The answer lies not in the technology itself, but in how we design the culture, talent, and strategic systems that surround it. This is where design leadership becomes business strategy.

Key insights include:

Culture is the Only Moat that Scales
Top researchers aren't chasing bigger compute budgets, but "clarity of purpose". They want to work somewhere that aligns with their deepest beliefs about what AI should become. Culture is designable infrastructure - creating environments where brilliant minds want to stay through psychological safety, autonomy, and meaningful work.

Build Systems, Not Buzz
What endures are feedback loops, decision systems, and research processes that help teams learn and adapt faster. Smart organizations should focus on:
- Research processes that turn failures into insights quickly
- Decision-making systems that pivot without losing momentum
- Knowledge-sharing practices that compound learning
- Feedback mechanisms that capture what's working

Designing AI's Understanding
Companies like Scale AI are selling "human judgment at scale" - the ability to encode meaning, context, and nuance into machine-learnable formats. This is fundamentally a design challenge about capturing the "why" behind human decisions.

Rethinking Talent as Product
The best AI researchers join for freedom, clarity, and philosophical alignment. The most magnetic teams have systems that help people do their best work, treating talent experience as a strategic product.

The Specialized Intelligence Approach
Instead of chasing artificial general intelligence, the author suggests focusing on "domain-specific intelligence that collaborates beautifully with humans" - systems that understand context and enhance human capabilities.

The core message is that the AI race isn't just about compute or models, but about designing the culture, teams, and long-term systems around them. "Every AI breakthrough becomes table stakes within months. Talent and trust? Those take years to build and seconds to lose."

## Agents of beauty
URL: https://martijnvanderdoes.com/#journal
Author: Martijn van der Does
Published: 2024-12-27
Section: Journal
Summary: Explores intersection of beauty, mathematics, and AI, arguing that true beauty requires human emotional understanding that algorithms cannot replicate.
Tags: beauty, mathematics, AI-limitations, human-emotion

Full Text:
Beauty is elusive. Designers seek it through form, proportion, and meaning, while mathematicians discover it in patterns and ratios. Yet as we explore the idea of training AI to recognise and create beauty, a tension emerges: can a model built on data and formulas truly grasp what makes something resonate with the human spirit?

The Mathematics of Art & Design

Some time ago I was asked whether it was possible for an AI agent to judge beauty. As someone deeply involved in design, this question resonated with me. After all, isn't beauty at the heart of what creative people strive for in their work?

It's not yet possible for any machine learning model to determine beauty. It probably never will be. The reason is that machine learning models are mathematical. They break things down into mathematics and store it as data in tokens - much like designers break down complex products into their basic elements and principles.

When you ask an AI agent to do something, it's like asking a designer to create without the context of human experience. The agent refers to its tokens - fragments of data and patterns - much as a designer might refer to knowledge of materials, forms and principles. But while a designer can draw on lived experience, emotional understanding and cultural context, the AI can only present what its training data suggests is statistically most appropriate.

Beauty Can Be Systematised Though?

Beauty can be systematised. Not only can maths be beautiful, but both man-made and natural art can and have been based on mathematical principles and formulae. Designers have long understood this - from the golden section to the rule of thirds, mathematical principles underpin much of what we consider beautiful design.

Consider the Fibonacci sequence found in nature - from the arrangement of leaves on a stem to the spiral of a pine cone. This mathematical pattern creates visual harmony and functional efficiency. While we can identify and replicate these mathematical principles of beauty, the real challenge lies in understanding why they resonate with us on an emotional level.

Enchantment in Interaction Design

In product design, mathematical precision is transformed into emotional resonance through thoughtful interaction. The most beautiful designs create moments of delight through the perfect marriage of form and function. When a user interacts with a well-designed object, they experience a sense of rightness, of inevitability.

Summary: Introduces Dynamic User Experiences (DUX) as a paradigm integrating AI with interface design, emphasizing human-AI collaboration over replacement.
Tags: DUX, interface-design, AI-collaboration, user-experience

Full Text:
The article explores Dynamic User Experiences (DUX), a design paradigm integrating artificial intelligence with user interface design. The key insights include:

1. AI Tools as Translators
The authors argue that AI design tools are more effective as "translators" between designers and non-designers, rather than direct design replacements. They suggest these tools can:
- Help non-designers provide precise design feedback
- Enable better visualization of design ideas

2. Dynamic User Experiences (DUX) Concept
DUX involves creating interfaces that:
- Use modular, adaptable UI components
- Dynamically reassemble based on user interactions
- Maintain a consistent underlying design structure

3. Complexity Simplification
The core philosophy is to "Simplify complexity" by creating intuitive interfaces with complex underlying systems. Drawing inspiration from video game design, the approach focuses on presenting information progressively and naturally.

4. Challenges and Considerations
The article highlights three primary challenges for DUX implementation:
- Accessibility
- Privacy protection
- Performance limitations of generative AI technologies

5. Future Vision
The authors envision a collaborative future where AI augments human creativity, not replaces it. The goal is to create digital experiences that are:
- Responsive
- Intuitive
- Respectful of user needs
- Technologically advanced yet familiar

The article concludes by emphasizing that the future of interface design lies in a balanced approach between human creativity and machine efficiency.

## The Algorithmic Aesthetic
URL: https://martijnvanderdoes.com/#journal
Author: Tim Groot, Martijn van der Does
Published: 2024-08-22
Section: Journal
Summary: Deep dive into design language evolving around AI integration, from Xerox Star to modern interface design and the sparkle emoji as AI signifier.
Tags: algorithmic-design, interface-history, AI-aesthetics, user-experience

Full Text:
The article traces the evolution of design in digital interfaces, focusing on how technology has shaped user experience over time. Key milestones include:

1. Xerox Star (1981): Introduced the "hamburger menu" and established early principles of user interface design. Designer Norm Cox emphasized that "every pixel mattered" and design should be "as elegant as it is simple."

2. Infinite Scroll (2006): Created by Aza Raskin to reduce user friction, with the philosophy that "the less a user's mind is cluttered with irrelevant decisions, the clearer their mind is to accomplish what they need to do."

3. Airspace (2016): A term coined by Kyle Chayka describing the homogenization of digital aesthetics through algorithmic curation.

4. AI Integration (2023-2024): The rise of the sparkle ✨ emoji as a universal symbol for AI features, representing a new stage in algorithmic design.

The article argues that design is not just about appearance, but about creating intuitive, user-friendly experiences. The "algorithmic aesthetic" represents an ongoing evolution of how technology interfaces with human perception and interaction.

A key insight is that design must balance functionality with appeal, making complex technologies feel accessible and even magical to users.

 Navigating Creative Blocks

Welcome to Edition #43 of the Creative Currents newsletter!

In this week's issue of Creative Currents, we cover the issue of overcoming creative blocks during our design and strategy process. Here, we asked our team what they do when they have hit a creative block and take you through the three key techniques for re-inspiring your creative process.

We also give you an inside look into Vera van de Seyp's creative process and insight on the role of generative AI in design. Finally, we cover how the Quiet Luxury trend may impact brands and take you through a new typeface developed by Amsterdam-based designer, Johnathan Castro.

Navigating Creative Blocks

In the fast-paced world of design, staying motivated and creatively inspired is crucial for producing exceptional (and timely) work. However, every designer and strategist, regardless of their experience, encounters creative blocks from time to time. These periods of stagnation can be endlessly frustrating, especially when we are on a deadline.

Three key techniques for overcoming creative blocks:

1. Seek Inspiration Beyond Your Category
Falling into a mood board spiral can happen to anyone, where every image or trend starts to blend together, stifling your creativity. Algorithms perpetually feed similar imagery and concepts, limiting perspective. Listening to lectures or panel discussions by passionate creatives from different disciplines can inspire new ideas.

2. Take a Walk for Fresh Perspective
Sometimes, the best way to break through a creative block is to step away from your workspace and go for a walk. Changing surroundings can help clear your mind and reset focus. During this time, you might stumble upon unexpected solutions and ideas.

3. Change Projects or Tasks
If you have multiple projects, consider switching between them when facing a creative block. Changing tasks can provide a mental break and allow you to approach your initial project with a fresh start. This shift in focus can help you see your work from a different perspective.

What these tips have in common is the need to take a break from your current work. Stepping away is not a waste of time, but an investment in creative well-being. By giving yourself space to recharge and explore new inspiration sources, creative blocks become temporary challenges.
